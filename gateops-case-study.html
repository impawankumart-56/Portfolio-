Overview

This project focused on improving vehicle verification, product validation, and approval workflows used at industrial plants.

Security teams were handling vehicle queues, material checks, and approvals manually under time pressure. The goal was to make the process faster, clearer, and less error-prone.

The Problem

The existing workflow depended heavily on:

  • manual verification,
  • paper records,
  • repeated coordination calls,
  • and unclear approval visibility.

This created:

  • slow vehicle processing,
  • verification mistakes,
  • delayed approvals,
  • and operational confusion during high-volume activity.
Workflow
Pre-Assign Vehicle → Vehicle Scan → Product Verification → Approval → Vehicle Release

The biggest issue appeared during product verification where speed and accuracy directly affected operational flow.

Design Approach
Faster Product Verification
Designed scannable product lists with: checkbox-based validation, clear selection states, mismatch indicators, and reduced visual noise. The experience was optimized for quick recognition and faster decision-making.
Structured Approval Experience
Designed a simplified approval screen with: vehicle details, driver information, product summary, and quick approve/reject actions. This reduced back-and-forth communication and improved decision clarity.
Role-Based Experiences
Security teams required: fast execution, quick scanning, and minimal interaction effort. Approvers required: structured visibility, review-focused layouts, and confident decision-making. The system was designed around these different operational needs.
UX Principles Applied
Reduce Cognitive Load Scannable Interface Design Error Prevention Decision Support Role-Based UX
Expected Impact
  • Faster vehicle processing
  • Improved tracking accuracy
  • Reduced approval delays
  • Better operational visibility
  • Lower manual verification effort
Reflection

This project reinforced that operational UX is about helping users make fast and accurate decisions under pressure.

In industrial environments, clarity and speed matter more than visual complexity.
